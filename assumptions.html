How We Work It Out

A plain-English guide to how we estimate the economic impact of the World Cup on each host city — what we measure, what we assume, and what we don't know.

Our estimate — extra money generated by visiting fans
$5.7bn
Additional economic activity from fans travelling to all 16 host cities across the USA, Canada and Mexico — spending on hotels, food, taxis, tickets and entertainment.

Imagine a fan flying from South Korea to watch their team play in Kansas City. They book a hotel for four nights, eat at local restaurants, take taxis, buy souvenirs and spend at the stadium. Every dollar they spend goes into the local economy — paying wages for hotel workers, restaurant staff, and taxi drivers, who then spend some of that money locally too. Our model counts up all that extra spending, across millions of fans and 104 matches, to arrive at a total economic impact for each city.

The key word is extra. Not all World Cup visitors are truly additional — some replace regular tourists who decided to avoid the city during the tournament. We account for this carefully, and it makes a big difference to the final numbers.

The Five Steps

We follow the same five steps for every city. Each step involves a key assumption — we explain all of them below.

1
How many fans visit?
We start with each stadium's official FIFA capacity and assume 95% of seats are filled — FIFA's own attendance projection. Of those fans, we count 25% as international visitors (people who flew in from another country) and 35% as domestic away fans (people who travelled from elsewhere in the same country). The remaining 40% are locals from the host city itself — their spending is mostly money they would have spent anyway, so we leave it out.

This means bigger stadiums generate more economic impact. Dallas's AT&T Stadium holds 94,000 people, so 9 matches there produces far more visitor spending than Toronto's BMO Field, which holds only 45,000.
📋 Sources: FIFA official stadium capacities (confirmed October 2025). FIFA 2022 World Cup attendance surveys for the 25%/35% visitor splits.
2
How much does each fan spend?
International fans spend $285–$437 per day depending on which city they're in — because a restaurant meal in Guadalajara costs far less than the same meal in San Francisco. We adjust each city's spending using Numbeo's Restaurant Price Index, which measures eating-out costs city by city (New York = 100).

On top of that, we add a 20% World Cup premium — prices for food, drink and entertainment are inflated during major sporting events. Domestic fans spend about 35% of what international fans spend, because they don't pay for international flights and tend to know better where to find good value.

We also separately count the extra hotel spend on match nights — some cities see hotel prices double on game days (Monterrey: +104%, Boston: +42%), while others barely move (Atlanta: +2%, Dallas: +3%).
📋 Sources: LendingTree 2026 World Cup Fan Cost Analysis. Numbeo Cost of Living Index by City (June 2026). GiveMeSport/SeatPick stadium beer prices. Lighthouse Hospitality hotel rate and match-day premium data.
3
Subtract the visitors who would have come anyway
This is the most important step — and what most optimistic economic forecasts get wrong by ignoring it. In a city like New York or Miami that already draws millions of international tourists every year, some World Cup fans simply replace regular tourists who chose to stay away. The city is busy either way, so the World Cup isn't truly creating new economic activity for every visitor.

We call this displacement, and we vary the deduction by city type. Cities like Kansas City or Guadalajara that rarely see international tourists get almost full credit for every World Cup visitor — they're genuinely additional. Cities like New York or Los Angeles that are always packed with tourists lose more credit.
📋 Sources: Zimbalist & Matheson NFL Super Bowl studies (20–40% displacement for major US cities). FIFA 2014 Brazil secondary city research (~15%). UK Sport/DCMS mega-event evaluation framework (10–35%).
4
Apply the economic multiplier
When a fan spends $100 at a hotel, that doesn't just benefit the hotel. The hotel pays wages to housekeepers and receptionists. Those workers spend their wages at local shops and restaurants. Those shops buy supplies from local wholesalers. The original $100 circulates through the local economy, creating more economic activity than the initial amount.

We use a city-specific multiplier — larger, more self-sufficient cities like New York (1.85×) generate more local circulation, while smaller or more import-dependent cities like Vancouver (1.10×) see more money leak out to suppliers in other cities or countries. The Canadian cities use a lower multiplier anchored to Deloitte Canada's published research finding of $1.09 generated per dollar spent.
📋 Sources: Deloitte Canada Sport Business Advisory (2024). Tourism Economics/Oxford Economics multiplier literature. World Bank tourism guidance for Latin American economies.
5
Show it as a % of each city's economy
A $500m impact sounds huge — but it means something very different for Kansas City (annual economy: $180bn) versus New York (annual economy: $2,100bn). For Kansas City it's 0.23% of their whole annual economy. For New York it's barely a rounding error at 0.03%.

By expressing impact as a percentage of annual GDP we put every city on an equal footing. This reveals the real story: smaller cities with lower international tourism see a proportionally far bigger World Cup effect than the big-name cities that dominate the headlines.
📋 Sources: US Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) Metropolitan Statistical Area GDP data. Statistics Canada CMA GDP. Mexico INEGI metropolitan area GDP. All figures converted to USD.
The Formula (In Plain Numbers)

For each city, the calculation looks like this:

Step 1 — Fan count per match:
  Stadium capacity × 95% occupancy × (25% intl + 35% domestic)

Step 2 — What they spend:
  Fan count × city-adjusted daily spend × 4 days stay
  + hotel match-night premium spend

Step 3 — Remove displacement:
  Total spend × (100% minus displacement rate)

Step 4 — Multiplier effect:
  Incremental spend × city multiplier (1.10× to 1.85×)

Step 5 — Divide by city GDP:
  GDP impact ÷ annual city GDP × 100 = % impact
The Numbers We Assume

Some numbers in our model come from published data. Others are our best estimates based on research. Here's every assumption we make, and where it comes from.

Stadium occupancy
95% of capacity
FIFA's own attendance projection for the 2026 tournament. Actual World Cup matches routinely sell out or near-sell out.
International fan share
25% of occupied seats
Based on FIFA 2022 Qatar attendance surveys. Means Dallas (94,000 capacity) hosts about 22,325 international fans per match; Toronto (45,000) hosts about 10,688.
Domestic away fan share
35% of occupied seats
Fans who travelled from elsewhere in the same country. They spend less than international visitors (no flights) but are still incremental to the host city.
Base international daily spend
$390/day at NYC prices
Derived from LendingTree's $2,100 total trip cost estimate. Adjusted down or up for each city's actual cost of living using Numbeo's Restaurant Price Index — so a fan in Guadalajara spends around $285/day while one in San Francisco spends ~$437/day.
Domestic daily spend
35% of international rate
Domestic fans skip the international airfare (often the biggest single cost) and tend to be more cost-savvy in familiar surroundings. Same city-specific cost-of-living adjustment applied.
World Cup price spike
+20% on food, drink & entertainment
Prices for food, drinks and entertainment rise during major sporting events. Stadium beer prices alone average $9–$13 per pint in the US, double normal pub prices. Applied on top of the city's normal cost level.
Hotel match-night premium
City-specific — 2% to 104%
From Lighthouse Hospitality Intelligence data. Ranges from barely-noticeable in Atlanta (+2%) and Dallas (+3%) to prices doubling in Monterrey (+104%) and Vancouver (+55%). Reflects genuine supply scarcity in smaller markets.
Average length of stay
4 days per visit
FIFA 2022 Qatar data showed international visitors stayed an average of 4.2 days. We use 4 days as a conservative round number. This assumption is monitored — real data from 2026 may lead to a revision.
Economic multiplier
1.10× to 1.85× — city-specific
How much each $1 of tourist spending ultimately generates in the local economy. Large US cities like New York (1.85×) score higher because money circulates more locally. Canadian cities score lower (1.10–1.15×) anchored to Deloitte Canada's published finding of $1.09 per dollar spent.
Displacement deduction
8% to 35% — city-specific
The share of World Cup visitors who replace tourists that would have visited anyway. Higher in tourism-heavy cities like New York (35%) and Miami (35%). Much lower in cities like Kansas City (8%) and Guadalajara (8%) where international tourism is rare.
City GDP figures
2025 estimates
US cities: Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) 2023 actuals, grown at each city's historical rate to estimate 2025. Canada: Statistics Canada, converted to USD at $0.72. Mexico: INEGI, converted at MXN 17.5 to $1.
Why Displacement Varies by City

Think about two extremes. New York City welcomes 9 million international visitors every year — it's one of the most visited cities on earth. When the World Cup is in town, some of those regular tourists simply don't come: they avoid the crowds, the high prices, and the disruption. The World Cup replaces some normal tourism rather than adding to it.

Kansas City, by contrast, gets around 120,000 international visitors in a normal year. When 18,000 international fans show up for a single match, those people are genuinely new to the city. There's almost nobody being replaced.

These tiers are calibrated against published academic research. NFL Super Bowl studies by economists Zimbalist and Matheson find 20–40% displacement for major US cities. FIFA's own Brazil 2014 research found around 15% for secondary cities. Our 8–35% range sits comfortably within these established findings.

How Our Number Compares to FIFA's

FIFA published a much larger headline figure for the economic impact of the 2026 World Cup. Our $5.7bn and FIFA's $22.3bn can both be correct — they're measuring different things.

What goes into each estimate
Our estimate — fan spending only
Hotels, food, taxis and entertainment spent by fans who travelled to attend matches. The most directly measurable component — and the most conservative.

FIFA's own spending on running the tournament
Prize money for teams, broadcasting costs, staffing, logistics and equipment. This money also flows into host-country economies.

$22.3bn

Our $5.7bn is actually very close to FIFA's own estimate of tourist spending alone (~$7.5bn globally, ~$5–6bn across the three host countries). The extra ~$16bn in FIFA's headline figure comes from counting their own operational spending, government investment in infrastructure, and complex ripple effects through the wider economy — all legitimate, but not the same thing as fans arriving in cities and spending money.

Independent economists are more sceptical of the headline numbers. Goldman Sachs (June 2026) studied every World Cup since 1982 and found the effect on national GDP is "marginally positive but statistically insignificant" in the long run. Professor Victor Matheson, who specialises in sports economics, said the US impact is "likely a fraction of what was being advertised." We think our conservative, footfall-focused approach gives a more honest picture of what actually lands in host-city economies.

What We Don't Know

Any economic model involves guesswork — and it's important to be honest about where ours is least certain.

⚠ The limitations of this model
  • These are estimates, not measurements. We don't have access to real hotel booking data, actual visitor spending, or live attendance figures. Everything is modelled from published research and reasonable assumptions.
  • We treat every match the same. In reality, a USA vs Mexico group game will draw very differently from a match between two smaller nations. High-profile matches will generate more international visitors; lower-profile ones fewer. A future improvement would vary visitor counts by match importance.
  • Hotel prices came down before the tournament. Dallas, Atlanta, Miami and Philadelphia cut hotel prices by up to a third as demand was lower than expected. Our hotel premium figures come from pre-tournament published data and may overstate the actual accommodation premium.
  • The displacement rates are judgement calls. We've calibrated them carefully against published research, but we can't directly observe how many regular tourists stayed away. They could be higher or lower than our estimates.
  • Canadian and Mexican figures are less precise. The US Bureau of Economic Analysis publishes detailed city-level GDP data. Statistics Canada and Mexico's INEGI are less granular, and both require currency conversion at exchange rates that fluctuate.
