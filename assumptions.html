How We Work It Out

A plain-English guide to how we estimate the economic impact of the World Cup on each host city — what we measure, what we assume, and what we don't know.

Total estimated economic impact across all 16 host cities
$7.7bn
Additional economic activity directly generated by the 2026 FIFA World Cup — above and beyond what would have happened anyway.

Think of it this way: when a fan flies from Japan to watch a match in Kansas City, buys a hotel room, eats out, and takes a taxi — that's money the city wouldn't have seen otherwise. Multiply that by millions of fans across 104 matches in 16 cities, and you get a very large number. But not all of it is truly extra — which is where our methodology gets interesting.

How We Calculate It

We work through five steps for each city. The maths is straightforward — the interesting part is the assumptions we make along the way (which we explain fully below).

1
Count the visitors
We estimate how many fans attend each match — 18,000 international (people who flew in from abroad) and 32,000 domestic (people who travelled within the country). Multiply by the number of matches at each venue.
Source: Stadium capacity data, FIFA projected occupancy ~70%, split 25% international / 45% domestic based on FIFA 2022 World Cup attendance surveys.
2
Estimate what they spend
International fans spend an average of $390 per day over a 4-day trip — covering flights, hotel, food, tickets, and local transport. Domestic fans spend $137 per day (35% of international) because they don't pay for international flights and tend to spend more carefully in familiar surroundings.
Sources: LendingTree 2026 World Cup Fan Cost Analysis ($2,100 total trip), SoFi stadium food/drink estimates, Lighthouse hotel rate data per city.
3
Deduct displacement
Not every World Cup visitor is truly extra. In a city like New York that already attracts millions of tourists, some World Cup fans replace regular tourists who chose to avoid the city during the tournament. We call this "displacement" and deduct it. Crucially, the displacement rate varies by city — see our tier system below.
Sources: Oxford Economics mega-event studies; Zimbalist & Matheson NFL Super Bowl displacement research (20–40% for major US cities); FIFA 2014 Brazil secondary city analysis (~15%); UK Sport/DCMS mega-event evaluation framework.
4
Apply the economic multiplier
When a tourist spends $100 at a hotel, that hotel pays wages to staff, who spend at local restaurants, who buy from local suppliers. The money circulates. We use a multiplier of 1.75× — meaning every $1 of visitor spend generates $1.75 of total economic activity.
Sources: Tourism multiplier literature range 1.5–2.0×. We apply the median 1.75× uniformly. City-specific multipliers (larger cities typically higher due to less economic leakage) are a planned future refinement.
5
Express as % of city GDP
A $500m impact means very different things to Kansas City ($180bn economy) and New York ($2.1tn economy). Expressing impact as a percentage of annual GDP puts every city on a level playing field — and reveals which cities are experiencing the biggest World Cup effect relative to their normal economic size.
Sources: US Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) Metropolitan Statistical Area GDP; Statistics Canada Census Metropolitan Area GDP; Mexico INEGI metropolitan area GDP. All converted to USD.
The Formula
Gross visitor spend =
  matches × (18,000 intl fans × $390/day + 32,000 domestic fans × $137/day) × 4 days
  + hotel match-day premium spend

Incremental spend = gross spend × (1 − displacement rate)

GDP impact = incremental spend × 1.75 multiplier

% of annual GDP = GDP impact ÷ city metro GDP × 100

City Displacement Tiers

This is where our methodology differs from most economic impact studies, which apply a single displacement rate to every city. We use four tiers based on how tourism-dependent each city already is. The more tourists a city normally gets, the more World Cup visitors are simply replacing those tourists rather than adding to the economy.

Global Gateway
35% displaced
65% incremental
NYC, LA, Miami, Vancouver, Mexico City
These cities are international tourism hubs year-round. A significant share of World Cup fans substitute for regular visitors who chose to stay away during the tournament.
Major Regional Hub
25% displaced
75% incremental
Boston, SF, Toronto, Seattle, Houston
Large cities with meaningful but not dominant tourism. Some crowding-out occurs but the city can absorb additional demand.
Mid-tier Host
15% displaced
85% incremental
Dallas, Atlanta, Philadelphia
Tourism exists but isn't the economic driver. Hotel data confirms minimal match-day premiums (2–3%) — plenty of room to absorb visitors.
Low-tourism City
8% displaced
92% incremental
Kansas City, Guadalajara, Monterrey
Minimal pre-existing international tourism. World Cup visitors are almost entirely additional — no meaningful counterfactual tourist is being displaced.

Academic grounding: NFL Super Bowl studies (Zimbalist, Matheson) find 20–40% displacement for major US cities. FIFA 2014 Brazil secondary city analysis found ~15%. UK Sport/DCMS mega-event framework uses 10–35%. Our range of 8–35% is consistent with this body of research.

What We Don't Know

Good methodology means being honest about uncertainty. Here's what could make our estimates wrong:

⚠ Limitations of this model
  • These are estimates, not measurements. We don't have access to actual hotel occupancy data, real visitor spend, or live attendance figures. The model uses reasonable assumptions based on published research.
  • Visitor volume is assumed, not counted. We use a flat 18k international + 32k domestic per match for all venues. In reality, a Mexico vs Brazil match will draw differently to a group stage fixture between lesser-known teams.
  • The multiplier is uniform. In reality, larger, more economically diversified cities generate higher multipliers. This is a planned refinement.
  • Displacement is tiered, not measured. We can't directly observe how many regular tourists stayed away during the World Cup. Our tiers are calibrated against academic research but are ultimately judgement calls.
  • Economic conditions can change. Hotel prices initially surged then dropped as demand was lower than expected (Atlanta, Dallas, Philadelphia cut rates by up to a third). Our hotel premium figures reflect pre-tournament published data.
  • Canadian and Mexican GDP figures are estimates. BEA data for US cities is authoritative. Statistics Canada and INEGI metro GDP figures are less granular and converted at spot exchange rates.
